Unit 2: Uncounted Voices — Archives, Infinity, and Abstraction (Weeks 4–6)

Mini-Project 2: "Hidden Figures of U of T"

Guiding Question: Which women have shaped mathematics here, and how do we know?

Math Focus: translating mathematical ideas for public audiences (exposition), logic of evidence.

Local Anchors: U of T Archives & Records Management Services (UTARMS) at the Thomas Fisher Rare Book Library, 4th floor—reading room access Tues–Fri for U of T and the public.

Core Question: Who are the mathematicians and students history forgot—and why?

What students do:

  • Research a little-known figure (or group) from the archives.
  • Create a mini-exhibit panel or zine page that includes:
    • A short biography (with archival evidence).
    • A visual math artifact (diagram, crochet model, or data visualization).
    • A "Why forgotten?" analysis (structural barriers, gender norms, etc.).
  • Deliverable: 1–2 panels or 4-page zine spread + sources.

    Unit 3: Public Voice – Hidden Figures & Belonging (Weeks 7–12)

    Capstone Project: This comprehensive capstone project invites you to create a public-facing artifact that illuminates "Hidden Figures" in U of T's mathematical history or explores themes of "Mathematics and Belonging" through a dynamic dialogue between archival findings and contemporary U of T perspectives. Regardless of your chosen path, your artifact must powerfully communicate a mathematical concept visually, designed to captivate and excite a public audience about the ideas you uncover. You will choose one of two distinct tracks to achieve this overarching goal:

    Track A: Exhibit/Zine (Hidden Figures of U of T)

    Discover and highlight contributions of individuals (particularly women or underrepresented groups) to mathematics or related fields *within the University of Toronto archives*. Your project should explore questions such as: "Why might these figures have been overlooked or forgotten?" "What systemic factors contribute to certain groups being forgotten in mathematics?" and "How can we, through this project, highlight their stories and mathematical impact more effectively?"

    Track B: Oral History Remix (Mathematics & Belonging: Then & Now)

    Explore personal narratives and experiences of belonging (or not belonging) in mathematics, specifically by creating a **dialogue between contemporary perspectives and historical accounts found in the U of T archives**. This track invites you to consider how mathematical spaces can be made more inclusive, bridging past and present experiences. Your deliverable will weave together *interviews with current mathematicians or math students in the U of T math department* with other archival materials or historical context. These interviews should not be hypothetical.

    Math Focus: Communicating Mathematics to a Public Audience Visually

    Your public artifact *must* incorporate a **visual representation and explanation** of a relevant mathematical concept.
